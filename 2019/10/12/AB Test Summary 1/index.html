This is a summary of article The pitfalls of A/B testing in social networks. More details can be found on this pages.

Let’s get started!

Background

Usually when we do AB testing, we will randomly divide users into two groups, and each of them will get a different version of product or website. Then we will look the differences of users’ behavior between two groups.

Unfortunately, this will not work if you are testing for a product, which highly relies on the interaction between users.

Limitation 1

Ex: if we are going to launch a new video-chat feature and want to test if people like it before launching it to all users. If we randomly split people into two groups, who would they use the feature with?

Two ways:

  • Allow people in test group to chat with anybody
  • Only allow people chat within the test group

Both of them have limitation.

For the first one,

  • People in control group will not be a real control group, since they are also exposed to this new feature.
  • Also, it is weird, frustrating, half-experience for people in control group

For the second way,

  • One good is that it will keep the control group free of the new feature.

  • For the testing group, the users’ experience will not be well now and it is an uneven experience. Since users are randomly split to two groups, they will not be able to chat with their friends who in control group or chat with anyone they want.

  • Lead to the changes of users’ behavior:

    • Not buy or use: They may not use the feature until it’s out of testing.
    • Buy or use: worse for both groups. The test group would restrict themselves to a small corner of the site, and the control group would have a bunch of ignored messages and unreciprocated love.

Limitation 2

We can measure “higher-order effects” or network effects. And this limitation will appear almost everywhere. These effects occur when the changes induced by a new feature leak out of the test group and affect behavior in the control group as well.

Ex: let’s say we wanted to test a new rule that required users to put 500 characters of text into their profile.

  • Users in testing group will have a better experience, since other users will know more about them.
  • This would change the experience for people in the control group — they’d see a sudden influx of users with interesting essays and also have an improved experience on the site because they’d find more interesting people that they want to message.

Theoretically, it is a clear win. But when we do AB test with per-user assignment, we will not see a clear win, since we looks for the difference between two groups.

Using per-community random assignment

Here, a “community” is any group of users whose interactions are primarily directed to other users within the same group.

So, how to define a community?

We will do it basing on social graph, which is a graph that represents social relations between entities. Basically, we want to use some mathematical methods to partition the social graph and find isolated, non-interacting groups.

Then we can use some graph partitioning algorithms like Normalized Cuts.

Defining geographic communities

In some situation, per-community will not work.

Ex: The social graph of dating app is different with others, since users focus on finding new people to talk to rather than maintaining contact with existing connections. So, the community is really defined by “anybody that’s near you” as opposed to “people you have a history of interacting with”.

Problems:

  • There is no official boundaries to define the region.
  • Different people have their own unique set of geographic boundaries.

Solve:

Take mathematical approach to defining ‘optimal’ regions by drawing boundaries that maximizes the number of connections within each region relative to the number of connections that occur across regions using Normalized Cuts.

Steps:

  • Define symmetric matrix $A_{N*N}$, where N is the number of locations. $A[i, j]$ means the number of messages were sent between the users in location I and location j.
  • Get degree matrix $D$. Get graph Laplacian $L=D-A$.
  • Do SVD for $L$ and get the eigenpairs of $L$. Each fully-isolated sets of nodes will be represented by an eigenvector with an associated eigenvalue of 0. Further segmentation of these regions is done by looking at the patterns in the eigenvectors with the k smallest, non-zero eigenvalues. The value of k can be varied depending on how many regions you want to make.
  • There is a dynamic map on the original website, you can check it.

Drawbacks of per community assignment

  • Need to make changes to our statistical analyses to reflect that we now have a nested experimental design.
  • Less statistical power.
