This is a summary of Seven Rules of Thumb for Web Site Experimenters. It talks about seven rules of thumb, which is quite useful I think.

Rule 1:Small Changes can have a Big Impact to Key Metrics

As we all know, a small bug can have a big negative impact on key metrics. So as positive impact. There are several examples in the paper.

But we should notice here that even though these small changes exist, they are very rare and surprising. And also, We can’t claim that these results will replicate to other domains, but these and other easy-to-run experiments may be still worth trying, in case they lead to a breakthrough.

Rule 2: Changes Rarely have a Big Positive Impact to Key Metrics

Two key points:

  • Key metrics are not some specific feature metric, as those are easy to improve, but an all-up organizational metric, such as Sessions/user and Time-to-success.

    • Ex: It is easy to significantly increase clicks to a feature (a feature metric) by highlighting it, but what really matters is the overall page CTR or overall experience. Many times, what we do is just shifting clicks around and cannibalizing other areas of the page.

  • Metrics should be diluted by their segment size. It is easy to improve in a small segment, but if it is diluted to the overall impact, it will be very small.

Intersting:

  • following taillights is easier than innovating in isolation.
  • Not to trust results that are too good to be true. Twyman’s law: Any figure that looks interesting or different is usually wrong! Like normal distribution, high value is far away from the center with very low probability, which usually due to bugs.

Rule 3: Your Mileage WILL Vary

Getting idea from others is great, but we should notice several problems with others’ case studies.

  • The quality of others’ case studies varies.
  • What works in one domain may not work in another.
  • Novelty and Primacy effects.
  • Misinterpretation of result. Effects are often attributed to a specific factor, or the underlying reason is not understood.

When reviewing results of experiments, ask yourself what trust level to apply, and remember that even if the idea worked for the specific site, it may not work as well for another. One of the best things we can do is to report replications of prior experiments.

Rule #4: Speed Matters a LOT

Even a slight delay to the page performance may impact key metrics in the Treatment.

The best way to quantify the impact of performance is to isolate just that factor using a slowdown experiment.

Key points for this experiment:

  • The slowdown quantifies the impact on the metric of interest at the point today. It will change if the site performance changes.
  • The experiment measures the impact of a slowdown. Then we can use it to adjust the impact of the feature, and answer the question of its impact if it were implemented efficiently.
  • Assessing the impact to key metrics if the site were faster, helping us evaluate ROI (Return-On-Investment) of such efforts. Using a linear approximation, we can assume that the impact of the metric is similar in both directions (slowdown and speedup).

Rule 5: Reducing Abandonment is Hard, Shifting Clicks is Easy

Increasing user engagement, or reducing abandonment, is considered positive, but it is a difficult metric to move. Most experiments show that there can be significant shifts in clicks from one area of the page to another, but abandonment rate rarely moves or moves very little.

But if the module simply cannibalized other areas of the page, it is only useful if those clicks are better, however “better” is defined for the site.

Phrased differently: local improvements are easy; global improvements are much harder.

Rule 6: Avoid Complex Designs: Iterate

Simple designs are best in the online world and given the many pitfalls, they are easier to understand, run sanity checks, and thus more trustworthy. A complex design is usually not only unnecessary, but can hide bugs.

Why simple:

  • online we have a continuous stream of users and we can use concurrent designs to run hundreds of concurrent experiments, testing one or two variables at a time.
  • Can align with agile software methodologies and building minimum viable products. Complicated MVTs that rely on a lot of new code tend to be invalid because bugs are found in the code for at least one of the variables.

We encourage our engineering teams to deploy new code quickly and use experiments to provide a form of exposure control: start with small 1% treatments, then ramp up if there are no egregious declines in key metrics.

Rule 7: Have Enough Users

Experimentation methodologies typically rely on the means, which are assumed to be normally distributed. (Central Limit Theorem )

Formulas for minimum sample size given the metric’s variance and sensitivity (the amount of change one wants to detect) provide one lower bound, but these assume that the distribution of the mean is normal. Our experience is that many metrics of interest in online experiments are skewed which may require a higher lower bound before you can assume normality.

Rule of thumb for the minimum number of independent and identically distributed observations needed for the mean to have a normal distribution is $355*s^2$, where s is skewness coefficient of the distribution of the variable X, defined as $s=\frac{E[X-E(X)]}{[Var(X)]^{3/2}}$

Recommendations:

  • use this rule when the |skewness| > 1
  • If the control and treatment are expected to have the same distribution, then make sure that the control and treatment are equally sized. If the split is equally sized (e.g., 50%/50%), then the distribution of the delta will be approximately symmetric
